Vivisection is surgery (dissection) conducted for experimental purposes. They are typically performed on living organisms, and in the case of Hare and Burke and with the help of Dr. Knox performed on humans. The goal of vivisection is to view the internal structure of living things. Vivisection in earlier times was used as a form of torture. In the 1820s in Scotland, vivisection was considered business on the black-market business, where people were killed and bodies dug up in the name of science. Simultaneously, the laws and regulations of vivisection on humans in the form of science were being regulated. Medicine was starting to go through a transformation. Hospitals and more advanced tools like the stethoscope, along with a mass of other developments, were causing medicinal practices to boom. Along with this, the advancement of policies regarding human life and medical practices needed to be improved. At the time, the main victims who were killed were not protected by Scottish law at the time. These were mainly those who died in prison, suicide victims, and the bodies of foundlings and orphans. With the rise in hospitals, there was also a rise in the prestigiousness of medical training. However, there was a lack of bodies for the study of this medicine. This, in turn, caused a rise in students, lecturers, and graver robbers (resurrection men) and began the black market of exhumed cadavers. During the 19th century the debate around vivisection was characterized as whether the suffering of animals and their rights or interest were less important than the effects of vivisection on the experimenter’s morals and reputation. It is argued that the 1876 Cruelty to Animals Act focus on the wantonness or deliberateness of vivisection encourages experimenters to justify their actions because of the perceived medical benefits of vivisection. On the other side, many physicians were concerned that the links between medicine and vivisection would deter patients from seeking help. In the nineteenth century there was a growing worry and fear around doctors and medical professionals, much of the fear was coming from women who would regularly be sent to doctor for hysteria or other demeaning “illnesses”. With the rise of vivisection and other bloody medical studies and the Burke and Hare case involving Dr. Robert Knox many people grew to despise vivisection for its more murderous and painful techniques. One infamous medical professional who was seen by many as a monster was François Magendie as well as his disciple Claude Bernard. Bernard believed that only properly controlled and rigorous animal experiments could provide reliable information about physiology and pathology. He also believed in the importance of ideas and theories to create hypothesis and proper safeguarding but that they were only useful if they were backed by a substantial amount of experimentation. At the beginning of the Victorian era there was no regulation for animal experimentation but many prominent figures, especially in British society were against these types of experiments. Because of the revelation of the horrors of medical practices at the time many started to criticize those like Magendie and Bernard. Magendie became the forefront of this anti-vivisectionist movement and was described as “an exceptionally cruel person who submitted animals to needless torture.” His public presentations of his experiments soon became notorious with the discovery of one where he was dissecting a dogs facial nerves while the dog was nailed down by each paw and was left like that overnight until a continuation of the dissection until the next day. One of his experiments was recorded by an American physician where it states, “Monsieur M. has not only lost all feeling for the victims he tortures, but he really likes his business. When the animal squeaks a little, the operator grins; when loud screams are uttered, he sometimes laughs outright.” When things like this become public you can only imagine the public’s reaction to this horror. Now we must understand that the use of anesthetics was not available at this time, but, the nailing of animals down while preforming on them live while they are clearly experiencing pain was not necessary. Bernard would go on to carry out his experiments where he would cut open conscious animals while slowly cooking them alive to study thermoregulation. Both Magendie and Bernard where against the study and vivisection of humans without the use of substantial experimentation on animals. They did not hold animals at the same regard as humans and found testing on humans to be immoral. In an Article from 1885 in The North American Review by Noah K. Davis he states that in medical journals like The Lancet that, “…we find that painful experiments are not at all infrequent. The opponents of the practice collect the telling cases, and describe them as sawing across the backbone, dissecting and irritating the great nerves, driving catheters along the veins and arteries, inoculating with dreadful diseases, cutting out parts of the intestines, stewing and baking in a stove, pouring boiling water into the stomach, boiling or freezing to death, and by mechanical processes reducing the brain to the condition of ‘a newly plowed potato-field’.” So it is no wonder that with this knowledge out there for the public that cases like that of Birke and Hare become infamous for its time.
